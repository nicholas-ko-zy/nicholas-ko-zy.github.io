# Separation In Transportation Planning

I was recently thinking about how we can make walking and cycling more seamless and comfortable in Singapore. I’ve always found Singapore’s sidewalks to be too narrow to hold a conversation next to someone. You’re either blocking someone coming in the opposite direction or the person behind who’s trying to overtake you. I wondered what was the general urban planning principle which would make travelling more seamless in cities. After reading Susan Handy’s Shifting Gears, I learnt that seamless travel is highly correlated with sufficient separation. Specifically, the separation of (i) different modes of travel and (ii) direction of travel. 

Why does separation matter? A mode of transportation, separated from other modes, achieves a higher level of speed and safety. Highways separate cars from other modes, making it unnecessary to have traffic lights. Furthermore, cars are separated from vehicles travelling in the opposite direction by a median barrier, making it safe for cars to go at high speeds in a single direction. Mixing modes bring together different speeds and more caution is required to avoid collision. It’s one reason why street intersections are more dangerous than freeways. Travelling in a car on a highway and in a train feels seamless. Trains are separated from roads by an above-ground or underground track and, just like highways, have separate tracks for each direction. On the other hand, Singapore’s sidewalks are for the most part, shared by cyclists and pedestrians. Sharing the sidewalk reduces the speed that cyclists can go at, and increases the stress of pedestrians by keeping them on constant lookout for cyclists to give way to.

I think walking and cycling would be easier in Singapore, if there were more separation between sidewalks and cycling paths. Off the top of my head, I believe there are two ways to increase separation between sidewalks and cycling paths. The first way is to increase the width of the sidewalk so that bicycles and pedestrians will naturally separate themselves. The main reason why pedestrians and cyclists can’t seem to agree to keeping to one side is because there isn’t enough space. Once you grant more space, I think people will fall into line rather quickly without enforcement. The second way is more drastic, it is planning cycling routes that don’t run parallel to streets as much as possible. Having cycling routes that don’t run beside roads is another way to add separation. When you have cycling routes that are separated from cars, you have less to worry about traffic lights at intersections, the same way that trains don’t have to worry about traffic lights. 

I looked at the city of Delft to compare their pedestrian and cycling infrastructure with Woodlands and Sembawang in Singapore. I chose Woodlands and Sembawang because together, they made up roughly the size of Delft. We can view both cities as made up tracts of land. As a cyclist or pedestrian, whenever you choose to go from point A to point B, your route can be split into one of three categories. 

1) Travelling along the perimeter of the tract 
2) Travelling between tracts 
3) Travelling within tracts. 

Each category has a slightly different interpretation of separation. For ‘travelling along the perimeter of the tract’, separation can be thought of as the separation between pedestrians and cyclists. For instance dedicated lanes around a housing tract, with wide enough width to accommodate both modes moving in opposite directions. For ‘travelling between tracts’, we’re interested in the separation between cars, cyclists and pedestrians. This can come in the form of bridges, underpasses or intersections. The more separation the better. For travelling within tracts, separation is typically not an issue since cars that turn into minor roads will become the minority now. Within tracts, drivers typically pay more attention to pedestrians and cyclists. For example, the concept of woonerf, translated to mean ‘residential grounds’, was created in Delft. Woonerf streets do not have dedicated cycling paths or pedestrian paths but they do have traffic calming measures like signs that tell drivers they are entering a shared space, making it clear that they are guests in a shared street. 

I’ve never had a layover in the Netherlands, much less stepped into the city of Delft. However, just from Google Street View and OpenStreetMaps, I can see that Delft performs better in separation for all three categories that your route falls into.

One, when travelling along the tract perimeter is made easier in Delft because the sidewalks are wider. Two, when travelling between tracts in Delft, the intersections put more distance between active mobility users and cars as compared to Singapore’s intersections. In Singapore, during peak hours, cyclists tend to cycle out of the dotted lines to avoid pedestrians but are in-danger of getting hit by on-coming traffic. Another point to add for travelling between tracts is that there are dedicated cycling routes that go above or underground from the road level. Admittedly, this would be more difficult to do in Singapore because it would be expensive to drill underground to make an underpass for every intersection in every neighbourhood, but it works well separating cyclists and pedestrians from the street level. A good example of how moving underground is better than driving in Singapore is the underground network in Orchard MRT station. And finally, travelling within tracts is would be easier if there were more paved roads at the same elevation. I am not sure why Singapore’s HDBs are elevated, but the way to access the HDB from the street level is via the roads which typically bring you to a gantry or taking the stairs, which bikes cannot go. A better form of separation to move people within tracts is to have entrances and exits with slopes for bicycles which reduces the elevations caused by curbs, drains etc. The Land Transport Authority has introduced what they call ‘bicycle wheeling ramps’ to move up and down staircases connecting HDBs to the street. I’ve used them once while crossing an overhead bridge, and although they’re better than carrying my bike, they’re still awkward to use and might block other people who’re walking in the opposite direction. Just imagine yourself pushing up a bike during peak travel hours like brunch time on a Saturday morning. On the other hand, the movements within tracts in Delft are more seamless because of the same elevation and clear paths to go through the tracts. (Compare Molenbuurt with typical HDB tract)
